Why this works when other things haven't

vs. Therapy

No appointment. No waiting room.

Therapy is a relationship with a trained professional. These practices are what you do at 11pm on a Tuesday when something is still sitting in your chest and the next session is three weeks away.

vs. Meditation apps

Outcome-mapped, not just calm.

Apps ask you to observe. These practices ask you to move through. Each one targets a specific emotional transformation. You know exactly what you are doing and what changes when you come out.

Structured practice, not more information.

Knowing about emotional regulation is not the same as doing it. Free content gives you concepts. This gives you a step-by-step protocol you work through on your own, at your own pace.

Common questions

What you might be wondering.

What exactly is a practice guide?

A downloadable PDF you work through on your own. Each guide targets one specific emotion and runs through three stages: finding the thought driving it, using the body to discharge it, and resetting before you move on. It is structured like a protocol, not a worksheet. You read, pause, and do. Whatever room you are in, whenever it works.

How is this different from meditation or journaling?

Meditation asks you to observe. Journaling asks you to reflect. These practices ask you to move through. Sitting with an emotion without a method to discharge it often makes it louder. The three-stage cycle completes something. Thought work addresses the cognitive layer, somatic release addresses what the body is holding, and the inner reset closes the loop. You come out different, not just calmer.

Do I need to do them every day?

The 10-minute practices are designed to be daily. Not because there is a rule, but because consistency is what builds the skill. You can also reach for one on demand: before a difficult conversation, after a hard meeting, when something surfaces that you do not have time to spiral into. Both uses are valid. Daily practice builds the baseline. On-demand is what makes it useful in the real moments.

Which practice should I start with?

Start with what is loudest. Pressure at work, a decision you keep circling, guilt you cannot set down. If you know what it is, go there. If nothing is obviously loud right now, the Emotional Load Assessment will identify where the most charge is sitting. It takes 8 minutes.

What is the library, exactly? Is it a subscription?

No subscription. The library is a growing collection of individual practice guides, each targeting a different emotion and its transformation. You buy the ones you need. The 10-minute practices are available now. Deeper 30-minute and 60-minute sessions are being added. New emotions, new depths. It grows alongside you rather than being a one-time purchase that goes stale.

I am skeptical. I have tried things like this before and they did not work.

Most wellness tools are either too vague to do anything with, or too demanding to sustain. These are short, structured, and built around a complete three-stage cycle rather than a single technique. The 10-minute starting point exists because trust has to be earned. Start with one. See what happens.

I already do therapy. Is this redundant?

No. Therapy is a relationship with a trained professional. These practices are a daily self-directed discipline you do between sessions. Or instead of scheduling another one for something you can work through on your own. They are built for the moments therapy cannot reach: the Wednesday at 11pm, the five minutes before a difficult call, the car ride home when something is still sitting in your chest.
