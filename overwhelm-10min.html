What's happening

Overwhelm is the feeling of having too much happening at once. Too many thoughts, tasks, or emotions asking for attention at the same time. It pulls you forward into what needs to be done, what might go wrong, what can't be dropped.

Nothing is wrong with you. Your system is responding to carrying a lot. This practice is here to help you come back into the present and create a little space again.

"I am safe in this moment."

A note before you begin. This practice only works when you actually do it, not when you read about it. The shift happens in your body, not in your head. Trust what you feel during it, even if it surprises you. Your body, mind, and intuition are all part of this. Let them lead.

Before you begin

A quiet place to sit
Something to write with
Something to track time
10 uninterrupted minutes

Optional: Lavender, bergamot, or frankincense essential oil. Choose one and inhale gently before you start. It signals to the nervous system that something is about to shift.

Practice overview

This practice works in sequence. First naming what's happening in your mind, then moving it through your body, then returning you to the present. Each step builds on the last. Don't skip ahead.

The practice

01

Thought Work

7 minutes

Between what's happening around you and how you're responding to it, there's a gap. That gap is where this whole practice lives. Most of the time, we skip right over it. Our brain generates a thought, the thought triggers a feeling, the feeling drives a behaviour, and suddenly it's a habit we didn't choose. Mindfulness is the practice of stepping into that gap long enough to see what's actually going on.

With overwhelm specifically, that gap has been flooded. Your brain has been running on autopilot in "doing mode" — active, scanning, trying to manage everything at once. Before you can look at any thought clearly, you need to shift into "being mode" for a few minutes. The breath is how we do that. And the feelings? They're not coming from your circumstances. They come from your thoughts about your circumstances. Two people can face the same to-do list, one is calm, and one is drowning. The list isn't the problem. The thoughts about it are. That's what we're going to work with.

1 Breathe In

Take three slow, deliberate breaths. Inhale for 4 counts. Exhale for 6.

This is not relaxation, it's a shift in mode. The longer exhale tells your brain you're not in danger right now. It moves you from automatic to aware. You are here. Let that be enough for this moment.

2 Find It In Your Body

Without trying to fix anything yet, ask yourself: Where in my body am I feeling overwhelmed right now?

Scan gently. Maybe it's a tightness in the chest. A buzzing or scattered feeling through your limbs. Shallow breathing, tension behind your eyes, your mind jumping from one thing to the next before it finishes any of them.

Notice it without labelling it as good or bad. Approach it with curiosity, like you're encountering it for the first time. Mindfulness asks us to recognise and accept the experience as it actually is — not fight it and not collapse into it. Just see it. That seeing is already doing something.

3 Write Your Thoughts

Set a timer for 60–90 seconds. Write down every thought your brain is generating right now. Don't edit or organise, just let it come out.

Read back what you wrote. These are sentences in your mind, not facts about your life. Left unchecked, thoughts like these turn into feelings, then behaviours, then habits. You're stepping into the gap right now. You're catching the thought before it runs the show.

4 Find a Thought You Can Believe

Look at your list. Find the thought carrying the most charge.

Current thought:

We're not forcing a positive replacement — your brain won't buy that, and it'll feel worse. We're practising what mindfulness calls "letting go" of the grip on this thought, loosening it just enough to allow something slightly more spacious in. Not untying the knot completely, just loosening it. A thought you can actually believe right now.

Try these on and see what fits:

  • "I have a lot to do. I can choose what comes first."
  • "I don't have to solve everything today."
  • "One thing at a time is enough."
  • "I've been here before, and I've found my way through."

My shifted thought (write one you actually believe):

Sit with it. Breathe. Notice if there's even a small release somewhere in your body when you think it. A little less chest tightness. A bit more air. That's what we're going for — not a miracle, just enough ease to start moving.

02

EFT Tapping

6 minutes

EFT (Emotional Freedom Techniques) involves tapping specific acupressure points on the face and body while staying present with the feeling you identified in step one. Research shows it reduces cortisol measurably. The mechanism is direct: tapping these points sends calming signals to the amygdala, interrupting the stress response at its source. I've found Brad Yates to be one of the clearest and most grounded guides for this work. His videos are ones I return to myself.

Hold the thought or feeling you identified in step one while you tap. You don't need to say every word perfectly. Just stay present until it ends. If the charge softens, notice it. If it doesn't, that's okay too.

03

Gratitude

1 minute

Simple and honest. That's all it takes.

This isn't about pretending the overwhelm wasn't real. It's about shifting your nervous system from a threat orientation toward the present. Genuine gratitude, not forced, changes what your brain is scanning for. Your body follows.

Write down 3 things you're genuinely grateful for right now. They don't need to be significant.

Examples:

  • Someone close to you who supports you
  • Having food, shelter, or a place to rest
  • Something you did today that supports your life

Once you've written them, take one breath. Let the sensation settle in your body, not just your head.

04

Close

1 minute

Set the tone for what comes next. Place one hand on your heart. Choose one statement that feels steady and believable right now. Not aspirational, just true.

  • "I'm allowed to take this one step at a time."
  • "I don't need to carry everything at once."
  • "I support myself today."

Take one slow breath to seal it. Then gently return to your day.

Practice complete

Overwhelm often comes from living too far ahead.

Today, you chose to come back to yourself. You don't need to hold everything at once. Return to this practice whenever the weight starts building again. It takes less time each time you do.

Creating inner balance, one small pause at a time.
