What this stage is

Stage 2 gave you awareness.
This is where you build from it.

For 42 days you have been learning to see yourself clearly. You can name what is happening in your body before your mind has caught up. You recognise the thoughts that tend to run your emotional states. You have begun to create space between what activates you and how you respond.

Stage 3 does not add a new tool. It returns the one you have been using — and asks you to run it on your actual life. Not on a prescribed emotion. On what is real and present today.

This is where the work deepens. The thought work reaches further — into inherited beliefs, into the areas of life where your patterns show up most. The somatic practices become longer and more specific. And for the first time, the practice includes intuition and integration: learning to sit with what you have found, and let it change how you move through your days.

"You spent 21 days clearing the noise. Then 21 days learning to see. Now you use what you see to build something that lasts."

Building inner coherence

The PERMA Framework — knowing what you are building toward

Stage 3 introduces a lens for measuring not just emotional regulation, but genuine wellbeing. The PERMA model identifies five areas that determine whether a person is functioning well — not just performing well. Use it as a weekly reflective tool, not a daily metric. Once a week, take five minutes with one area.

High-achievers frequently score well on Accomplishment while running quiet deficits in Positive Emotions, Relationships, and Meaning. The practice is not to fix this as a project — it is to begin paying attention to what has been quietly neglected.

P
Positive Emotions
What are you actually savouring?

Not about staying positive. It is about noticing genuine satisfaction, pleasure, and ease when they appear — and not moving past them so quickly they never register. Many high-achievers experience good moments briefly then return immediately to what needs to be done. The practice is to slow down enough to let them land.

  • What brings me relief, and what brings me real renewal?
  • When do I feel simple pleasure without guilt or urgency?
  • What part of me still believes rest, joy, or softness has to be earned?
  • What did I learn growing up about happiness that I no longer want to live by?
E
Engagement
Where were you fully present?

Engagement is being fully absorbed in something — not just busy, but genuinely present. High-achievers often confuse high-volume activity with engagement. Being stretched and exhausted is not the same as being in flow.

  • What activities make me feel more like myself while I am doing them?
  • Where have I confused busyness with aliveness?
  • What do I keep saying I do not have time for that actually matters to me?
  • If I stopped performing usefulness for one week, what would I want to give my attention to?
R
Relationships
Which connections are real?

Genuine connection — where both people are actually present and honest — is one of the strongest predictors of wellbeing. Many high-achievers are skilled at performing connection while privately feeling disconnected. This area looks at quality, not volume.

  • Who lets me feel most unguarded, and what is different there?
  • Where do I over-explain, over-give, or become who I think I need to be?
  • What relationship pattern am I repeating because it feels familiar rather than right?
  • What would being more truthful in a relationship look like this week?
M
Meaning
What are you doing this for?

Meaning is the sense that what you are doing matters — not just to your metrics, but to something larger than personal achievement. Without it, even significant achievement tends to feel hollow. Go underneath output and image. Ask what gives your life weight in the best sense.

  • What matters to me even when no one else sees it?
  • Where have I built my life around urgency instead of meaning?
  • What part of my life feels full, and what part only looks full?
  • If I trusted that my worth is not dependent on constant output, what would I make more room for?
A
Accomplishment
Which wins were actually yours?

There is a difference between achieving something you chose and achieving something someone else needed you to want. This is not about doing less — it is about doing the right things. Redefine success from the inside instead of inheriting it whole.

  • What does success actually mean to me now?
  • Which goals feel chosen, and which ones feel inherited, rewarded, or expected?
  • What am I still trying to prove, and to whom?
  • What would accomplishment look like if it did not cost me my nervous system?
Practice structure

The same sequence. Deeper application.

The four-stage format does not change. What changes is the depth at which you enter it. The check-in is now a full inquiry. The thought work reaches the belief, not just the thought. The somatic practices are longer. And the intuition close — new to Stage 3 — asks you to listen to what the practice is telling you.

Optional — weekly focus

Choose one area of life to work with this week.

The daily practice works on whatever is present. But if you want to go deeper in a specific area — where your patterns show up most, or where you want to build more capacity — you can set a weekly focus. This shapes the thought work prompts for that week.

Optional — select one or leave blank
◈ Work & Performance
Performance · Achievement · Pressure

Where are you performing for an audience you have never chosen to perform for? This area looks at the gap between what you do and what you are doing it for.

  • What would I do differently this week if no one was watching?
  • Am I working toward something I chose, or something I was supposed to want?
  • Where am I measuring my worth by my output?
  • What fear is driving my work right now — and is it actually useful?
  • What would it mean to do good work without needing to prove it?
◈ Relationships & Connection
Belonging · Presence · Honesty

Many high-achievers are skilled at the performance of connection while carrying the weight of disconnection privately. This area looks at how you actually show up with people.

  • Which connections feed me and which deplete me — and am I honest with myself about that?
  • Am I present with the people who matter, or managing internally while appearing engaged?
  • What do I need from others that I have been too capable to ask for?
  • Where am I performing composure in a relationship that deserves something more honest?
  • What would change if I was less controlled and more real with one person this week?
◈ Identity & Self-Worth
Self-Trust · Beliefs · Who You Are

The beliefs driving your self-evaluation were mostly installed before you had the capacity to question them. This area looks at the difference between who you are and who you were taught to be.

  • When I am most critical of myself, what standard am I applying — and where did it come from?
  • Who would I be if I stopped needing to prove I was enough?
  • What parts of myself have I been hiding because they did not fit the acceptable version?
  • What do I actually value — separate from what I have built or been told to value?
  • What does self-trust feel like in my body? When did I last experience it clearly?
◈ Energy & Wellbeing
Recovery · Body · Sustainable Pace

Most high-achievers treat the body as a vehicle for performance rather than the intelligence guiding it. This area looks at what your body has been telling you that your mind has been overriding.

  • What signals has my body been sending that I have ignored to keep going?
  • When did I last genuinely rest — not recover to perform again, but rest without agenda?
  • What belief is underneath my relationship with rest? Is slowing down dangerous in some way I have not examined?
  • What does my energy feel like at the start of the day versus the end? What is creating that gap?
  • What one change would protect my energy rather than spend it?
◈ Purpose & Direction
Meaning · Values · What Matters

Many people arrive at high levels of external success and discover the emptiness that comes from optimising for the wrong target. This area looks at what you are actually building your life toward.

  • What am I doing that connects to something larger than achievement or approval?
  • If my performance metrics disappeared tomorrow, what would still feel worth doing?
  • What have I been postponing until I have achieved enough to feel it is earned?
  • What version of my life feels most like mine — not the most impressive, but the most true?
  • What would I regret not having done or said?

The practice

30 minutes. Four stages. Every day.

Work through the steps in sequence. Do not read ahead before completing the step you are in.

Your body knows what is happening before your mind has language for it. The check-in is not a warm-up — it is the foundation of everything that follows. Two minutes here makes the difference between a practice that lands precisely and one that floats over the surface.

AWhere are you holding it?

Before you name an emotion, locate it physically. Scan from feet to head. Notice where there is tension, weight, heat, numbness, or constriction.

  • Jaw, neck, shoulders — the load and the bracing
  • Chest — what is tight, hollow, or heavy here?
  • Stomach and gut — the anxiety lives here before the mind names it
  • Hips and lower back — what have you been carrying without acknowledging?
BName the emotion

Match the sensation to an emotion. Be specific — "anxious" and "scared" both race the heart, but they need different things. Use the Emotions and Sensations Chart if you need it.

CWhat thought is running underneath that?

Write the exact sentence your mind is running. Not the edited version — the real one. One to two lines.

Common at Stage 3: "I've done the work and I still don't trust myself." "I know what to do but I keep choosing the harder way." "Something still feels off and I don't know why."

At Stage 3 the thought work goes deeper. You are not just identifying the thought — you are tracing it back to the belief that generates it, and asking the harder question: did you choose this, or was it installed?

AWork with the thought

Choose one prompt that matches what came up in the check-in. Write honestly. Do not edit for coherence.

  • What specifically am I afraid will happen if I do not handle this perfectly?
  • Is this a fact I am responding to, or a story I have been rehearsing?
  • What am I trying to prove — and who am I proving it to?
  • What would I need to believe about myself for this thought to feel true?
  • What would I tell someone I love who was carrying this thought right now?
  • What does this thought cost me when I act from it?
BInherited or chosen?

Most beliefs driving high-achiever behaviour were installed before there was capacity to evaluate them. A parent's standard, a school environment that only rewarded performance, an early experience of conditional approval. The belief was adopted to stay safe, loved, or successful in that context. The question now is whether it still serves the life being built.

Inherited — often sounds like
  • "I need to do more to be valuable."
  • "Rest means I'm falling behind."
  • "Asking for help shows weakness."
  • "Being emotional is unprofessional."
  • "My worth depends on my output."
  • "If I stop pushing, I'll lose everything I built."
Chosen — often sounds like
  • "I work hard because it matters to me, not to prove anything."
  • "My capacity grows when I recover."
  • "Trusting others is how I build something sustainable."
  • "My feelings make me more precise, not less."
  • "Who I am exists separately from what I achieve."

Write the belief you found in the check-in. Then ask: Where did I first learn this was how I needed to be? What was I trying to stay safe from when I adopted it? Is this belief still true for the life I am building now? What would I choose to believe instead?

CIf you set a weekly focus — use it here

Return to the focus area you selected above. Use one prompt from that section. Write without editing. This is journaling, not performance.

The body holds what the mind has been processing. Thought work creates clarity. Somatic release creates discharge. The insight alone is not enough. You need to move it through.

At Stage 3 the practices are longer and more specific. Choose the one that matches what you found in Step 1. Let the body confirm the choice — a slight exhale when you read the right option, a pull toward it.

Extended Breathwork
5–10 minutes

Coherence breathing (5 counts in, 5 out) or the 4-7-8 pattern (inhale 4, hold 7, exhale 8). Set a timer and do not stop early. The nervous system shift happens in the sustained practice. Use for anxiety, pressure, high alert, or a racing mind.

Shaking & Discharge
5–7 minutes

Feet hip-width, knees slightly bent, let the body vibrate gently from the ground up. Increase if it wants to. This is how the nervous system naturally discharges accumulated stress. Use for overwhelm, held frustration, or stored physical pressure.

Qigong Flow
7–10 minutes

Slow arm swings, spinal waves, weight shifting with breath. The form does not need to be perfect — the intention does. Use for heaviness, disconnection, or when the body needs to move without intensity.

Body Scan Meditation
10 minutes

Move attention slowly from feet to head, pausing to notice sensation without trying to change it. This is a practice of presence. Noticing without fixing is how the nervous system processes what it has been carrying. Use for dissociation, numbness, or chronic low-level stress.

Guided Visualisation
7–10 minutes

Close your eyes and build a clear inner resource — a place, a version of yourself, a felt sense of safety. Use specific sensory detail. This trains the nervous system to access a regulated state on demand. Use for uncertainty, self-doubt, or when the future feels threatening.

Values-Based Embodiment
5–7 minutes

Choose one value you want to embody today. Stand in a posture that physically represents it — not performed, but genuinely felt. Breathe from that posture for 5 minutes. Use when identity or purpose work needs to move from idea to felt sense.

Duration matters at Stage 3. The practices above take the full time listed. Set the timer and stay in it. The nervous system shift happens in the sustained practice, not at the beginning.

This stage does not appear in Stages 1 or 2. It is specific to Stage 3. After thought work and somatic release there is a window — brief but real — where the nervous system is regulated and the mind is quieter than usual. This is where inner signal can be heard. You are not looking for inspiration. You are practising the skill of listening to what you already know.

AReturn to stillness

Sit quietly. Feet on the floor. One hand on your chest if that helps. Three slow unguided breaths. Notice whatever is present without trying to direct it.

BHold one question

Choose one and hold it without reaching for an answer. Let whatever arises arrive in its own time.

  • "What do I actually know about this that I have not been willing to say out loud?"
  • "What is the next honest step — not the safest one, the honest one?"
  • "What is the part of myself I have not been listening to?"
  • "What does my body know that my mind is still arguing with?"
CWrite what arrived

Do not filter. If nothing arrived, write that too. The practice of listening is worth more than the content of what is heard.

DOne grounded statement. One next step.

Choose the statement your body can actually receive — not the most impressive, the most true.

  • "I can move forward without having all the answers."
  • "I do not need external approval to trust what I already know."
  • "Slowing down is a form of precision, not avoidance."
  • "I can hold the discomfort of this and still act clearly."
  • "My performance is not the measure of my worth."
  • "I have built something real. I can trust it."

Then write your one next step. One concrete action you will take today.

Day 63

You built an inner world.
Now comes the work of keeping it.

Most people never build what you have built. They manage their emotional states from the outside — through wins, approval, and activity — and they never stop long enough to know what is happening on the inside. You spent 63 days doing the other thing.

What you do not maintain returns to its default. High alert is the nervous system's natural setting when nothing is being done to override it. Inner balance is not a destination you arrive at — it is the discipline that overrides the default, every day.

The library is how you maintain what you built. When a specific state activates — the kind that is familiar, that costs you when left unattended — you open the practice designed for exactly that state. No construction required. The work is already there.
