What's happening

Uncertainty feels threatening because the mind is built to anticipate. When it can't find a clear outcome to prepare for, it generates anxiety instead. The scanning doesn't stop. The alertness stays on.

The body tightens. The breath shortens. Decisions become harder. Not because you lack information, but because your nervous system has decided the absence of certainty is itself a threat. This practice is here to remind your body that you are safe in the present, even without knowing what comes next.

"I can meet what comes. I don't need to know it first."

A note before you begin. The mind will try to solve its way through this practice. Let it observe instead. The grounding in step two works through the body, not through reasoning. Stay with the physical sensations. They're doing the work.

Before you begin

A quiet place to sit
Something to write with
Something to track time
A steady object nearby
10 uninterrupted minutes

Optional: Frankincense or cedarwood essential oil. Both support grounding when the mind is scanning ahead.

Practice overview

Uncertainty is not a problem to solve. It's a state to move through. This practice uses journalling to name what the mind is doing, then uses the body to interrupt the scanning and return you to the present moment.

The practice

01

Thought Work

7 minutes

One of the things mindfulness teaches is that we live most of our lives in "Mind Full" mode — our brain generates thoughts automatically, those thoughts create feelings, those feelings drive behaviours, and the whole thing runs without us choosing any of it. With uncertainty, that automatic chain looks like this: something unknown arises, your brain predicts danger, anxiety spikes, and you spend the day spinning through "what ifs" trying to get control. Each loop makes the habit stronger.

The practice we're doing here creates a pause in that chain. Between the "I don't know" and the anxiety spiral, there is a gap. In that gap, you can see the thoughts your brain is generating, question whether they're facts or predictions, and find something more grounded to stand on. Mindfulness also teaches us that most of our suffering around uncertainty comes from the story on top of the not-knowing — not from the not-knowing itself. By practising non-judgment and acceptance (seeing things as they actually are), we can relate to the unknown differently. Not as a threat, but as something that hasn't been decided yet. We start with the breath because uncertainty keeps the mind spinning toward the future. The breath brings you back to right now, which is the only place you can actually do anything from.

1 Breathe In

Take three slow breaths. Inhale for 4 counts, exhale for 6. With each exhale, say quietly: "Right now, I am okay."

Say it because it's true — not because everything is figured out. This is the shift from doing mode to being mode, from scanning for what might happen to simply being here, in this breath, in this moment. That's enough to start with.

2 Find It In Your Body

Without trying to fix anything, notice: Where am I feeling uncertainty or anxiety right now?

Maybe it's a low hum of unease in your gut. A restlessness that makes it hard to sit still. A jumping mind, eyes that keep scanning, a feeling of not being able to land.

Approach it with a beginner's mind — as if you're noticing it for the first time, with curiosity rather than dread. Resisting a feeling keeps it activated. Recognising it, without judging it, is what starts to slow it down.

3 Write Your Thoughts

Set a timer for 60–90 seconds. Let your brain say everything it's been generating — the predictions, the fears, the what-ifs.

Read them back. You're being a scientist of your own mind. These are automatic thoughts your brain produced to try to protect you — not facts about the future. Not one of them is certain, including the worst ones.

4 Find a Thought You Can Believe

Find the thought carrying the most anxiety.

Current thought:

Now look for a thought that anchors you in yourself — in your own capacity and what's actually true right now — rather than one that tries to predict or control the outcome. This is the practice of "letting go": not of your awareness or your care, but of the tight grip on a prediction that isn't serving you. We're not going for false certainty. We're looking for ground.

Some options to try:

  • "I don't know how this will turn out, and I can handle it either way."
  • "I've navigated uncertainty before. I'm still here."
  • "I don't need to know the outcome to take the next step."
  • "I trust myself more than I trust the fear."

My shifted thought (write one you actually believe):

Breathe with it. Notice if the scanning, restless quality in your body settles even slightly. That's your body responding to a thought that feels more like ground than like falling.

02

Body Grounding

4 minutes

When the mind is scanning ahead, the body loses its sense of where it is right now. Proprioceptive grounding, simply pressing into the floor and noticing physical sensation, pulls attention back into the present. It interrupts the scanning at the physiological level, not just the cognitive one.

Sit or stand with both feet flat on the floor. Press them gently but firmly downward.

Notice the weight of your body. Notice the surface beneath you. Notice the temperature of your hands.

Hold the steady object. Feel its weight and texture. Let your attention rest on what is physically here, right now.

If the mind pulls forward, gently return to sensation. You don't have to think your way back. Just feel what is under your feet.

Stay here for a few minutes. There is nothing to figure out in this moment.

03

Presence Breath

2 minutes

A slow, extended exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system and signals safety to the body. It's one of the fastest ways to shift from threat state to present state. You don't need to calm down. Just breathe into what's here.

Inhale slowly through the nose for 4 counts. Hold for 4. Exhale through the mouth for 6 to 8 counts.

Repeat for several rounds. Let the exhale be longer than the inhale each time.

Notice what is present. Notice what is stable. Notice that you are here.

04

Close

1 minute

Place one hand on your heart. Choose one statement that feels grounded and true right now.

  • "I don't need to know what comes next to be okay right now."
  • "I can respond when something actually happens."
  • "This moment doesn't need my vigilance."

Take one slow breath. Then gently return to your day.

Practice complete

You don't need to predict what's coming next to be okay right now.

Today, you let your system stop scanning for a moment. That matters. You can meet things as they arrive. Return to this practice whenever uncertainty starts pulling you out of the present.

Creating inner balance, one grounded pause at a time.
