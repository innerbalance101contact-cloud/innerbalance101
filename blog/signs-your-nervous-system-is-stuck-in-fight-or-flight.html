Nervous System

5 signs your nervous system
is stuck in fight-or-flight

18 June 2026 · 7 min read

← All posts

You wake up exhausted. You snap at small things. You lie down to rest but your mind refuses to slow down.

You tell yourself to relax. But your body does not get the message.

This is not a willpower problem. It is a nervous system problem.

Most high-achieving professionals are operating in a low-grade state of chronic fight-or-flight without knowing it. Your stress response was designed for short bursts of danger. When it gets stuck on, it quietly drains your health, your focus, and your capacity to feel okay.

Here are five signs that your nervous system is stuck in fight-or-flight, and what is actually happening beneath the surface.

Sign 1: You cannot wind down at night

You close the laptop. You put your phone down. But your brain keeps running.

Replaying conversations. Planning tomorrow. Reviewing what went wrong. It feels like a browser with too many tabs open.

This is a hallmark sign of a dysregulated nervous system. When your sympathetic nervous system is chronically activated, your body maintains elevated cortisol levels into the evening. Cortisol is meant to peak in the morning and decline by night. When that rhythm is disrupted, your brain interprets rest as unsafe.

Your system is not being dramatic. It genuinely cannot find the off switch.

What this means for your body

Chronic nighttime activation suppresses melatonin production, disrupts deep sleep stages, and keeps your heart rate variability low. Over time, this accelerates burnout and weakens your immune response.

Sign 2: You are always braced for something to go wrong

You get a text from your manager and your stomach tightens before you even open it. You finish a big project but cannot feel relief, only the pressure of what comes next.

This is anticipatory stress, and it is a direct output of a nervous system that has learned to stay ready. When the threat response is chronically active, your brain begins scanning constantly for the next danger. Even in safe situations, your body runs threat assessments in the background.

Researchers refer to this as hypervigilance. It is exhausting. And it feels completely normal to people who have lived inside it long enough.

The hidden cost: Hypervigilance consumes significant cognitive energy. It narrows your attention, reduces creative thinking, and keeps prefrontal cortex function suppressed. In plain terms: you are burning through mental bandwidth just staying alert.

Sign 3: Your digestion is off

Bloating. Irregular bowels. Nausea before meetings. Feeling full quickly or never quite full enough.

Your gut and nervous system are directly connected through the vagus nerve, the longest nerve in your body. It links your brain to your digestive system and plays a central role in regulating your calm state, known as the parasympathetic response.

When fight-or-flight dominates, digestion gets deprioritized. Blood flow redirects to your muscles and heart. Digestive motility slows or becomes erratic. The gut microbiome itself changes in response to sustained stress hormones.

Digestive symptoms that have no clear medical cause are often the body communicating what the mind has not yet processed.

What to look for

  • Frequent bloating or cramping after ordinary meals
  • Changes in bowel regularity tied to stressful periods
  • Appetite loss or increased appetite as a stress response
  • Nausea in anticipation of difficult conversations or events

Sign 4: Small triggers produce big reactions

Someone interrupts you and you feel a disproportionate surge of irritation. A minor change in plans and you feel the floor shift.

This is nervous system reactivity. When your baseline activation is already high, there is less buffer between stimulus and response. The window of tolerance, your capacity to stay regulated under pressure, has narrowed.

It is not that you are an angry or anxious person. It is that your system is operating close to its limit most of the time. A small addition tips you over.

Many high-achieving people internalize this as a character flaw. It is not. It is a physiological state.

The window of tolerance

Developed by trauma researcher Dan Siegel, the window of tolerance describes the zone in which you can process stress without becoming overwhelmed or shutting down. Chronic stress shrinks this window. Nervous system regulation expands it.

Sign 5: You feel tired but wired

This is one of the most described experiences among people with dysregulated nervous systems. Deep fatigue paired with an inability to rest. Your body is depleted but still switched on.

This happens when your HPA axis, the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal system that governs your stress hormones, has been running for too long. Cortisol begins to fluctuate irregularly. You may feel flat in the morning when you need energy, and activated at night when you need rest.

This is sometimes called adrenal dysregulation, though the more precise framing is HPA axis dysfunction. It is not about adrenal exhaustion in the clinical sense. It is about a feedback loop that has lost its rhythm.

Common reports

  • Waking between 2am and 4am with a racing mind
  • Energy crashes in early afternoon
  • Feeling most alert after 9pm
  • Coffee required to feel baseline functional

What can you do about it

The first step is recognition. These are not personality traits. They are physiological patterns that can shift with the right input.

Nervous system regulation is a skill. It can be learned. It works not by forcing yourself to relax, but by giving your system the cues it needs to feel safe.

This is the foundation of the work at Inner Balance 101. The approach is evidence-informed, grounded in polyvagal theory, and built for people who live at high intensity.

If you recognize yourself in more than two of these signs, your emotional load may be higher than you realize.

Frequently asked questions

What does it feel like when your nervous system is in fight-or-flight?

Common experiences include a tight chest, shallow breathing, irritability, difficulty concentrating, and an inability to rest even when tired. Over time, these symptoms become so normalized that many people no longer recognize them as stress responses.

Can your nervous system get stuck in fight-or-flight permanently?

Not permanently, but chronically. The nervous system has neuroplasticity, meaning it can reorganize and recalibrate. Without targeted input, however, a hyperactivated baseline can persist for years. Consistent nervous system practices create new patterns over time.

How long does it take to regulate a dysregulated nervous system?

This varies significantly based on the duration of dysregulation, underlying load, and practice consistency. Many people begin noticing shifts within two to four weeks of consistent regulation practices. Deeper patterns typically change over three to six months.

Is fight-or-flight the same as anxiety?

Fight-or-flight is the physiological mechanism. Anxiety is one of its psychological expressions. Not everyone with a dysregulated nervous system identifies as anxious. Some experience it primarily as fatigue, irritability, numbness, or physical symptoms.
