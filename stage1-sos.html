The SOS

Stop the spiral. Come back to yourself. Ten minutes a day for 21 days.

21 Days
10 Min daily
Any time When
Day 22 Clarity unlocks
What this stage is

Stage 1 is not about going deep.
It is about getting stable.

Overwhelm is the feeling of having too much happening at once. Too many thoughts, tasks, or emotions all asking for attention at the same time. It pulls you forward into what needs to be done, what might go wrong, what cannot be dropped.

Nothing is wrong with you. Your system is responding to carrying a lot. This practice is here to help you come back into the present and create a little space again.

The commitment is 10 minutes. Daily. 21 days to build the foundation. Stage 2 unlocks on Day 22.

Before you begin. This practice only works when you actually do it. The shift happens in your body. Your body registers what your mind is still processing. This practice works in sequence: sensation first, emotion second, thought third. Follow it through.

When to do it

Three windows. Each one works differently.

◎
Morning

You haven't entered the day's noise yet. Your nervous system is rested. This is when you build the habit, before anything external competes for your attention.

◑
In the spiral

The moment you feel overwhelm peaking. You don't need to wait for a scheduled time. Two minutes of this practice is enough to interrupt the pattern before it runs.

◙
Evening

Close out what you carried. You are not replaying or planning. You are naming what the day activated and setting it down. What you do not process before sleep, your nervous system continues to hold.

The daily structure

Four parts. Same every day.
Ten minutes total.

This practice works in sequence. You name what's happening in your mind, move it through your body, return to the present, and close. Each step builds on the last. Do not skip ahead.

01
Thought work
Stop the autopilot
2 minutes

Between what's happening around you and how you respond to it, there is a gap. That gap is where this whole practice lives. Most of the time we skip right over it. A thought triggers a feeling, the feeling drives a behaviour, and it becomes a habit you didn't choose. Mindfulness is the practice of stepping into that gap long enough to see what is actually going on.

With overwhelm, that gap has been flooded. Your brain is running on autopilot in doing mode: active, scanning, trying to manage everything at once. Before you can look at any thought clearly, you need to shift into being mode for a few minutes. The breath is how you do that.

1 — breathe in

Take three slow, deliberate breaths. Inhale for 4 counts. Exhale for 6.

The longer exhale tells your brain you are not in danger right now. It moves you from automatic to aware.

2 — find it in your body

Without trying to fix anything yet, ask yourself: Where in my body am I feeling overwhelmed right now?

Scan gently. Maybe it's a tightness in the chest. A buzzing through your limbs. Shallow breathing, tension behind your eyes, your mind jumping before it finishes anything. Notice it without labeling it. Approach it with curiosity, like you're encountering it for the first time. That seeing is already doing something.

3 — write your thoughts

Set a timer for 60 seconds. Write down every thought your brain is generating right now. No editing. No organising. Just let it out.

Read back what you wrote. These are sentences in your mind, not facts about your life. You're stepping into the gap right now, catching the thought before it runs the show.

4 — find a thought you can believe

Look at your list. Find the thought carrying the most charge.

Current thought:

We are not forcing a positive replacement. Your brain won't buy that, and it will feel worse. We are loosening the grip on this thought just enough to allow something slightly more spacious in. A thought you can actually believe right now.

Try these on and see what fits:

  • "I have a lot to do. I can choose what comes first."
  • "I don't have to solve everything today."
  • "One thing at a time is enough."
  • "I've been here before and found my way through."

My shifted thought:

Sit with it. Breathe. Notice if there is even a small release somewhere in your body when you think it. A little less chest tightness. A bit more air. That is what we are going for.

02
EFT Tapping
Move it through the body
6 minutes

EFT (Emotional Freedom Techniques) involves tapping specific acupressure points on the face and body while staying present with the feeling you identified in Step 1. Research shows it reduces cortisol measurably. The mechanism is direct: tapping sends calming signals to the amygdala, interrupting the stress response at its source.

03
Gratitude
Return to the present
1 minute

This isn't about pretending the overwhelm wasn't real. It's about shifting your nervous system from a threat orientation toward the present. Genuine gratitude changes what your brain is scanning for. Your body follows.

04
Close
Set the tone for what comes next
1 minute

Place one hand on your heart. Choose one statement that feels steady and believable right now. Not aspirational. Just true.

  • "I'm allowed to take this one step at a time."
  • "I don't need to carry everything at once."
  • "I support myself today."

Take one slow breath to seal it. Then gently return to your day.

· · ·
After 21 days

The spiral still starts.
But you know what to do.

Overwhelm often comes from living too far ahead. Today, you chose to come back to yourself. Return to this daily. The shift becomes permanent when the practice does.

When you reach Day 22, Stage 2 opens. That is where you learn to see what has been underneath the overwhelm all along.
